Executive Summary

Analysis of the Chemistry Department's 21 faculty salaries reveals 3 salary inversions and significant compression between ranks. New assistant professors hired at $71K+ now exceed tenured faculty with 15+ years of service. Immediate action is recommended to address inversions, with a phased approach to resolve compression over 3-5 years.

Definitions

Salary Inversion
When a lower-ranked or less senior faculty member earns more than a higher-ranked or more senior colleague. Example: New Asst. Professor earning more than tenured Assoc. Professor.
Salary Compression
When the salary differential between ranks or experience levels is ≤ 9%, failing to appropriately recognize seniority and career progression. Industry standard differentials: 25-35% between ranks.

Recommendations

Proposed Faculty Senate Resolution

  1. Establish a Salary Inversion Review Committee with faculty representation to annually identify and recommend corrections for inversions.
  2. Request CUPA benchmark data for Chemistry (CIP 40.05) at R2 public institutions in the Southeast region for external market comparison.
  3. Propose a dedicated equity pool separate from annual merit increases, starting at $15,000-$20,000 annually for the Chemistry department.
  4. Implement a phased approach:
    • Year 1: Correct all rank inversions (Assistant > Associate)
    • Year 2: Correct experience inversions (New hire > Senior)
    • Years 3-5: Address compression systematically
  5. Establish transparent methodology using the formula: Adjusted Salary = Comparison Salary + $200 + ($100 × years in rank)
Framing for Administration: This is a retention risk issue, not a grievance. Replacing a senior faculty member costs 1.5-2× annual salary in search, startup, and lost productivity. A $15K investment protects a multi-million dollar institutional investment in faculty development.
